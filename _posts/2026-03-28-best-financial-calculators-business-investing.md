## 1. Texas Instruments BA II Plus

**The undisputed king of financial calculators.**

The TI BA II Plus is the most widely used financial calculator in the world, and for good reason. It's approved for the CFA, CFP, GARP FRM, and CAIA exams. It's the standard in business schools. And it's the go-to calculator for financial professionals who need to crunch numbers quickly and reliably.

The BA II Plus handles all the core financial calculations you'll ever need:

- **Time Value of Money (TVM):** Present value, future value, payment amounts, interest rates, and number of periods
- **Cash Flow Analysis:** Net present value (NPV) and internal rate of return (IRR) for uneven cash flows
- **Amortization:** Loan payment breakdowns by principal and interest
- **Statistics:** Mean, standard deviation, linear regression
- **Bond Pricing:** Yield to maturity, clean/dirty prices

The interface uses **algebraic entry** (the way you normally think about math), which makes it intuitive for anyone coming from a standard calculator. The five TVM keys (N, I/Y, PV, PMT, FV) become second nature after a few days, and once you've internalized them, you can evaluate any financial scenario in seconds.

Build quality is solid for the price point. The keys have good tactile feedback, the screen is clear, and the battery lasts for years. It's not a luxury item — it's a workhorse.

**Who it's for:** CFA candidates (it's one of only two calculators allowed), business students, financial analysts, and anyone who wants a reliable all-purpose financial calculator. If you're studying [investing](/best-investing-books-long-term-wealth/), this is the calculator you practice with.

**Key specs:** Algebraic entry, 10-digit display, 10 memories, NPV/IRR for up to 24 uneven cash flows, amortization worksheets, bond worksheet, depreciation worksheet.

## 2. HP 12C Financial Calculator

**The legendary calculator that's been on Wall Street desks since 1981.**

The HP 12C is the longest-produced calculator in history. It's been in continuous production since 1981 — over 40 years — and it remains the gold standard for real estate professionals, mortgage bankers, and financial veterans. There's a reason for its longevity: it's built like a tank and does exactly what financial professionals need.

The HP 12C uses **Reverse Polish Notation (RPN)** instead of algebraic entry. RPN eliminates the need for parentheses by entering operands before operators. Instead of typing "2 + 3 =", you type "2 ENTER 3 +". It feels alien for about two days, then becomes faster and more efficient than algebraic entry — especially for chained calculations where you're building on previous results.

Financial capabilities mirror the BA II Plus: TVM calculations, cash flow analysis (NPV and IRR), amortization, bond pricing, and depreciation. Where the HP 12C shines is in its **programming capability** — you can store up to 99 program lines for repetitive calculations. Real estate investors who evaluate dozens of properties use custom programs to speed up their analysis.

The build quality is exceptional. The HP 12C has a satisfying heft, crisp key presses, and a reputation for surviving drops, spills, and decades of daily use. Many financial professionals use the same HP 12C for their entire career.

**Who it's for:** Real estate investors, mortgage professionals, and anyone who wants the prestige calculator of the finance world. If you're studying [real estate investing](/best-real-estate-investing-books-beginners/), this is the calculator your mentors used. Also approved for CFA exams.

**Key specs:** RPN entry, 10-digit display, 20 memories, NPV/IRR for up to 80 cash flows, 99-line programming, amortization, bond calculations, 10+ year battery life.

[Buy on Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B00006IQCM?tag=kingledger20-20)

If you're choosing between the standard HP 12C and the Platinum, the Platinum is worth the modest price premium for the algebraic option alone. It future-proofs you — learn RPN at your own pace without being forced into it from day one.

## FAQ

**Can't I just use a spreadsheet or app instead?**

You can — and for complex modeling, you should. But a physical calculator is faster for quick calculations, works without internet, is allowed on exams, and builds mathematical intuition. The best approach is both: calculator for quick decisions, spreadsheet for detailed analysis.

**What is RPN and should I learn it?**

Reverse Polish Notation enters operands before operators (e.g., "5 ENTER 3 +" instead of "5 + 3 ="). It's faster for chained calculations and eliminates parentheses entirely. There's a 2-day learning curve, then it becomes second nature. If you're going to use a financial calculator daily, learning RPN is worth it.

**Is the BA II Plus Professional worth the upgrade?**

The Professional adds more memory and a few extra functions, but for most users the standard BA II Plus is sufficient. The Professional is mainly worth it if you're doing advanced CFA Level II/III work or need the extra cash flow storage.

**Do these calculators hold their value?**

The HP 12C especially holds value — used ones sell for nearly as much as new ones because of their durability and cult following. It's one of the few electronics where "vintage" is a feature, not a flaw.

**Can I use a financial calculator for real estate investing?**

Absolutely — that's one of the primary use cases. Calculating mortgage payments, comparing loan terms, evaluating property cash flows (NPV/IRR), and running cap rate analysis are all faster on a financial calculator than in a spreadsheet. Read up on [real estate investing](/best-real-estate-investing-books-beginners/) for the knowledge, then use the calculator for execution.

**Which calculator for the CFA exam specifically?**

Only two calculators are approved: the TI BA II Plus (including Professional) and the HP 12C (including Platinum). Most CFA candidates choose the TI BA II Plus because its algebraic entry has a shorter learning curve and most CFA prep materials (Kaplan, Mark Meldrum) use it in their examples.
